## Market Pattern

The competitor set clusters into five product types:

- Social cataloging: Goodreads, The StoryGraph, Literal, Hardcover
- Community-led reading: Fable, Literal, The StoryGraph book clubs or buddy reads
- Personal reading habit trackers: Bookly, Basmo
- Content ecosystems: Kindle, Libby
- General habit systems: Streaks, Habitica, Finch, Loop Habit Tracker

Reading Momentum should not try to become a full catalog, store, ebook reader, or public review network in the MVP. The stronger opening is a focused private-group habit layer: help people keep reading, reflect briefly, and support each other without requiring everyone to read the same book.

## Table-Stakes Features

These features appear repeatedly enough that Reading Momentum should assume users may expect them, even if the MVP implements only the smallest useful version:

- Book status tracking: want to read, currently reading, read, and did not finish.
- Flexible progress logging: pages, percentage, time, chapters, sessions, or format-aware check-ins.
- Reading goals: yearly, monthly, weekly, or individual goals.
- Streaks or consistency indicators.
- Basic reading stats and recaps.
- Shelves, lists, or lightweight collections.
- Notes, highlights, quotes, or reflections.
- Social visibility: friends, followers, clubs, groups, or feeds.
- Spoiler-aware discussion for shared reading contexts.
- Goodreads import or at least a future migration path.
- Privacy controls for what is shared.
- Reminders or routine-setting.
- Mobile-first access.

MVP implication: Reading Momentum should include only the table stakes needed to prove the core loop: current book, check-in, progress unit, weekly momentum, private group view, supportive reactions, and a small reflection prompt. Import, robust cataloging, public reviews, large clubs, and rich statistics can wait.

## Competitive Gaps

### 1. Supportive Private Accountability

Many competitors support public social graphs, clubs, followers, or community feeds. The gap is a calmer product built around small trusted groups where participation is visible but not shame-based.

Product implication: Default to private groups, limited group size, gentle check-ins, and recognition for encouragement. Avoid public ranking in MVP.

### 2. Consistency Over Raw Volume

Reading products often emphasize books completed, pages, challenges, or streaks. Habit apps emphasize daily completion. The gap is a scoring model that rewards returning to reading without over-rewarding speed or book count.

Product implication: Use a weekly momentum score based on sessions and consistency, with optional volume bonuses and diminishing returns.

### 3. Cross-Format Fairness

Readers use print, ebook, audiobook, academic PDFs, dense nonfiction, and rereads. Competitors vary in how much they support format-aware progress.

Product implication: Support minutes, pages, chapters, percent, and sessions from the start. Do not make book completion the only meaningful success state.

### 4. Reflection Without Full Reviews

Goodreads-style reviews are public and high-friction. Basmo and Literal support notes/highlights. The gap is quick, private or group-safe reflection that helps users deepen reading without writing a full review.

Product implication: Offer short prompts after check-ins, with optional sharing to the group. Prompts should encourage original thought, not summaries.

### 5. Recovery After Missed Reading

General streak apps often reset streaks. Reading Momentum can differentiate by treating missed days as normal and helping users restart.

Product implication: Use grace days, comeback badges, and weekly goals. Avoid copy that frames missed reading as failure.

### 6. Low-Noise Social Design

Fable and Literal show community, feeds, and clubs. Goodreads has a broad network and review graph. The gap is a focused feed that answers, "Who read recently, who needs encouragement, and what are we discussing?"

Product implication: MVP group feed should prioritize check-ins, reflections, encouragement, and weekly summaries, not open-ended content posting.

### 7. Trustworthy AI Boundaries

Basmo positions AI as a book chat and summary tool. Kindle is adding AI reading assistance according to secondary coverage that still needs direct Amazon confirmation. The gap is AI that supports reflection without replacing reading or generating risky judgments about users.

Product implication: AI in Reading Momentum should stay narrow: spoiler-aware prompts, note organization, and optional discussion starters. Do not launch AI summaries, AI-generated user judgments, or AI-written reflections in MVP.

## Implications For MVP

Build first:

- Private groups with invite-only membership.
- Current book tracking with flexible progress units.
- Fast check-ins: read today, progress amount, optional note.
- Weekly momentum score that privileges consistency over raw volume.
- Grace days and comeback states.
- Supportive group feed with reactions and short comments.
- Spoiler-aware reflection prompts, especially when users read the same book.
- Basic DNF support so users are not punished for abandoning books.
- Minimal stats: weekly sessions, active days, current streak or momentum, group activity.
- Privacy controls for what check-ins and reflections are shared.

Defer:

- Public discovery network.
- Full review platform.
- Large public clubs.
- Bookstore, ebook reader, or library borrowing.
- Complex recommendation engine.
- Rich catalog management.
- Deep AI chat with books.
- Broad social feeds.
- Competitive leaderboards ranked by pages or books completed.

Avoid:

- Shame-based streak resets.
- Page-count-only leaderboards.
- AI-generated judgments about user taste or identity.
- Designing for screen time instead of reading time.
- Requiring a whole group to read the same book.

## Differentiation Statement

Draft positioning:

Reading Momentum is a private reading accountability app for friends who want to read more, whether or not they are reading the same book. It rewards consistency, gentle comeback progress, and supportive discussion instead of public reviews, page-count competition, or noisy feeds.
