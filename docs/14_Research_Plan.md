# Research Plan

This plan defines how Reading Momentum should gather evidence for major product decisions. Evidence gathering is pending until sources can be verified. Do not treat any research claim in this document as cited support until a source is added and reviewed.

## Research Standard

Preferred evidence should come from credible, traceable sources:

- Peer-reviewed academic papers and systematic reviews
- Official reports from libraries, publishers, schools, universities, and literacy organizations
- Platform documentation from Apple, Google, browser vendors, accessibility standards bodies, and notification providers
- Direct competitor pages, help centers, app store listings, product screenshots, and public pricing pages
- User research conducted with target users, with method, sample, and limitations documented

Every completed research note should include:

- Source
- Key finding
- Product implication
- Risk or limitation
- Decision affected
- Success metric

## 1. Habit Formation

Research questions:

- What makes a reading habit more likely to persist beyond the first few weeks?
- How should goals be framed: time, pages, chapters, sessions, streaks, or flexible weekly targets?
- When do streaks motivate users, and when do they create guilt or abandonment?
- What kinds of recovery mechanics help users return after missing days?

Preferred source types:

- Peer-reviewed behavior change and habit formation research
- Research on implementation intentions, self-monitoring, cues, rewards, and habit discontinuity
- Digital behavior change intervention reviews
- User interviews with readers who have tried and failed to maintain reading routines

Product decisions informed:

- Goal-setting defaults
- Streak and grace-day rules
- Comeback states after missed reading
- Weekly consistency scoring
- Onboarding prompts and recurring rituals

Success metrics:

- Week 1 activation rate
- Week 4 reading check-in retention
- Percentage of users who recover after missing a goal
- Average reading sessions per active user per week
- User-reported pressure, guilt, or motivation after missed sessions

Evidence status: Pending.

## 2. Social Accountability

Research questions:

- What forms of accountability increase reading without making users feel judged?
- Are small private groups more effective than large public communities for early retention?
- Which social signals are helpful: check-ins, reactions, comments, nudges, shared goals, or group summaries?
- How should the product handle uneven participation within a group?

Preferred source types:

- Academic research on social support, accountability, group motivation, and online communities
- Reports on book clubs, reading groups, and community literacy programs
- Direct competitor analysis of group features in reading and habit apps
- Interviews with book club members and casual friend groups

Product decisions informed:

- Private group model
- Group size recommendations
- Social feed design
- Nudges and reminders between members
- Recognition for encouragement, not just volume
- Controls for muting, privacy, and opt-out behavior

Success metrics:

- Group creation-to-first-check-in rate
- Percentage of groups with at least three active members after four weeks
- Number of supportive interactions per active group
- Retention difference between solo users and group users
- User-reported comfort sharing progress

Evidence status: Pending.

## 3. Reading Behavior

Research questions:

- How do readers define meaningful progress across print, ebook, audiobook, dense nonfiction, fiction, and academic reading?
- Which tracking units feel fair across different formats?
- How often do readers want to reflect, discuss, or log progress?
- What barriers most commonly interrupt reading momentum?

Preferred source types:

- Library, publisher, literacy, and reading behavior reports
- Academic studies on reading motivation and leisure reading
- Surveys of target users
- Interviews across different reading formats and reading identities

Product decisions informed:

- Supported progress units
- Fairness rules for scoring
- Reflection prompt timing
- Reading session logging design
- Book metadata needs
- Accommodation for audiobooks and non-linear reading

Success metrics:

- Percentage of check-ins using each progress unit
- Completion rate for reading sessions started
- User-reported fairness of scoring
- Reflection response rate
- Reading minutes or sessions reported per week

Evidence status: Pending.

## 4. Gamification

Research questions:

- Which game mechanics support the core reading behavior instead of distracting from it?
- Should points reward consistency, volume, reflection, discussion, encouragement, or completion?
- How can leaderboards avoid discouraging slower readers?
- What rewards should be temporary, personal, group-based, or seasonal?

Preferred source types:

- Peer-reviewed gamification and motivation research
- Research on intrinsic motivation, autonomy, competence, and relatedness
- Competitor pages showing streaks, points, badges, challenges, leaderboards, and achievements
- User tests comparing score explanations and dashboard states

Product decisions informed:

- Momentum score formula
- Badge and achievement system
- Leaderboard presence, ranking logic, or absence
- Weekly recap design
- Reward copy and visual hierarchy

Success metrics:

- Repeat check-in rate after earning a reward
- Distribution of points across reading styles
- Percentage of users who understand the score
- Percentage of users who report the score feels motivating
- Drop-off rate after low-score weeks

Evidence status: Pending.

## 5. Notifications

Research questions:

- What notification timing and frequency help users read without feeling interrupted?
- Should reminders be scheduled by user intent, group activity, inactivity, or reading routine?
- Which notification types should be push, email, in-app, or silent digest?
- How should users control reminders during busy weeks, travel, exams, or vacations?

Preferred source types:

- Apple, Google, browser, and email platform documentation
- Research on prompts, reminders, interruption, and notification fatigue
- Accessibility guidance for notifications and motion
- Competitor notification settings and onboarding flows
- User testing of notification copy and cadence

Product decisions informed:

- Reminder defaults
- Permission request timing
- Notification settings
- Quiet hours and pause controls
- Group activity digests
- Re-engagement campaigns

Success metrics:

- Notification opt-in rate
- Reminder-to-check-in conversion rate
- Notification disable rate
- Uninstall or churn after notification bursts
- User-reported helpfulness of reminders

Evidence status: Pending.

## 6. Accessibility

Research questions:

- What accessibility requirements apply to a mobile-first reading habit web app?
- How should the app support screen readers, keyboard navigation, reduced motion, contrast, font scaling, and cognitive accessibility?
- What design patterns make check-ins fast and understandable for users with low energy, attention constraints, or assistive technology?
- How should progress visualizations expose equivalent text?

Preferred source types:

- WCAG guidance and official accessibility documentation
- Apple, Google, and web platform accessibility documentation
- Inclusive design research
- Audits using assistive technologies
- Feedback from users with accessibility needs

Product decisions informed:

- Component design standards
- Color and contrast rules
- Form design
- Progress visualization alternatives
- Motion and animation limits
- Notification and reminder accessibility

Success metrics:

- WCAG conformance target pass rate
- Keyboard-only task completion
- Screen reader task completion
- Contrast issue count
- Form error recovery rate
- Accessibility issue severity from audits

Evidence status: Pending.

## 7. Competitor Review

Research questions:

- How do reading apps and habit apps position themselves?
- Which features are table stakes, differentiated, or overbuilt?
- How do competitors handle groups, streaks, challenges, reviews, book metadata, recommendations, and paid plans?
- What trust, privacy, and moderation patterns appear in social reading products?

Preferred source types:

- Direct competitor websites, app store pages, help centers, pricing pages, and public product documentation
- Screenshots captured during product walkthroughs
- Public changelogs, release notes, and support articles
- Official social media or blog announcements from competitors
- User reviews only as directional signals, not primary evidence

Initial competitor set:

- Goodreads
- The StoryGraph
- Fable
- Literal
- Bookly
- Basmo
- Kindle
- Libby
- Hardcover
- StoryShots or Blinkist, for summary-app contrast
- Habit apps such as Streaks, Habitica, Finch, and Loop Habit Tracker

Product decisions informed:

- MVP feature boundaries
- Differentiation around supportive private groups
- Scoring and challenge model
- Pricing and packaging hypotheses
- Onboarding language
- Privacy and social sharing controls

Success metrics:

- Clear competitor feature matrix completed
- MVP feature list revised based on evidence
- Differentiation statement tested with target users
- User preference ranking versus competitor concepts
- Conversion from landing or prototype tests

Evidence status: Pending.

## Research Sequence

1. Define target user segments and recruiting criteria.
2. Build a source library with verified sources only.
3. Complete competitor review from direct product sources.
4. Run five to eight exploratory interviews with readers in small groups, casual book clubs, and solo reading routines.
5. Convert findings into decision briefs for scoring, groups, notifications, and onboarding.
6. Test prototype flows with target users.
7. Add source-backed decisions to the decision log.

## Open Research Risks

- Habit research may not transfer cleanly from general health or productivity behaviors to reading.
- Reading progress is hard to compare fairly across formats, genres, and difficulty levels.
- Social accountability can become pressure if the product overemphasizes public comparison.
- Gamification may increase check-ins without increasing real reading.
- Notifications may improve short-term return behavior while harming trust if overused.
- Competitor user reviews can be biased and should not replace direct product inspection or user research.
