# Growth Positioning

Status: Draft for PM review. Do not sync to GitHub until approved by PM.

This document translates the current product strategy into launch positioning, early-adopter hypotheses, channel ideas, onboarding messages, and promise boundaries. It is grounded in the existing vision, product principles, roadmap, and competitive analysis.

## Core Positioning

Reading Momentum is a private reading accountability app for friends who want to read more consistently, even when everyone is reading different books.

The product should be positioned as:

- More habit-focused than Goodreads.
- More social and supportive than a solo reading tracker.
- Lighter and more flexible than a traditional book club.
- More reading-specific than a generic habit app.
- Less noisy than public social feeds or large reading communities.

Short positioning options:

- Read more with friends, without needing a formal book club.
- A private reading group that helps everyone keep going.
- Build a reading habit with friends, at your own pace.
- Stay consistent with reading, even when everyone picks their own book.
- Gentle accountability for people who want reading to stick.

Preferred draft:

Reading Momentum helps small groups of friends build consistent reading habits through quick check-ins, flexible goals, and supportive accountability.

## Target Early Adopters

### 1. Friend Groups That Keep Saying They Should Read More

Why they fit:

- They already have trust and existing social ties.
- They may not want a formal shared-book club.
- They benefit from visible progress without public pressure.

Likely trigger:

- New year, summer, semester start, post-grad routine change, or a shared desire to reduce passive screen time.

Launch message:

- Start a low-pressure reading group where everyone can read their own book.

### 2. Casual Readers Restarting A Dormant Habit

Why they fit:

- They identify as readers but have lost consistency.
- They may feel overwhelmed by long reading goals.
- They need comeback mechanics more than strict streaks.

Likely trigger:

- "I used to read more" moments, screen-time fatigue, or a new book they are excited about.

Launch message:

- Get back into reading with small check-ins and gentle weekly momentum.

### 3. College Students And Young Professionals

Why they fit:

- Reading competes with school, work, social life, and screen time.
- Friends may be remote or on different schedules.
- They are familiar with lightweight tracking and habit apps.

Likely trigger:

- Semester start, winter break, graduation, new job, or book recommendations from friends.

Launch message:

- Keep reading with friends, even when schedules and books do not match.

### 4. Remote Friends

Why they fit:

- They need rituals that maintain connection across distance.
- Reading is a shared identity, but synchronous meetings are hard.
- A private feed can be useful without demanding long conversations.

Likely trigger:

- Group chats that already exchange book recommendations.

Launch message:

- Turn your book group chat into a quiet reading accountability circle.

### 5. Self-Improvement Readers Who Dislike Harsh Streaks

Why they fit:

- They already understand goals, streaks, and dashboards.
- They may be burned out by rigid habit apps.
- They may respond to weekly progress and grace days.

Likely trigger:

- Wanting a calmer habit system tied to a meaningful activity.

Launch message:

- A reading habit tracker that rewards consistency without punishing missed days.

## Non-Initial Audiences

These audiences may matter later, but should not define the MVP launch:

- Public creator-led reading communities
- Libraries and schools
- Corporate learning groups
- Large public book clubs
- Authors and publishers
- Heavy review writers
- Users who primarily want recommendations or a full book catalog
- Users who need a built-in ebook reader or library borrowing workflow

MVP implication: Early growth should target small trusted groups and habit formation, not public content networks or institutional sales.

## Differentiation

### Against Goodreads

Goodreads is strong for cataloging, reviews, discovery, and public social proof. Reading Momentum should not compete head-on there.

Differentiation:

- Private groups instead of a broad public review graph.
- Check-ins instead of full reviews.
- Weekly consistency instead of total books or review activity.
- Supportive reactions instead of public comparison.

### Against The StoryGraph, Literal, And Hardcover

These products are closer to modern reading trackers and Goodreads alternatives.

Differentiation:

- Habit loop first, catalog second.
- Small-group accountability first, public discovery second.
- Reading momentum score instead of primarily taste, stats, or shelves.
- Flexible group reading where each person can read a different book.

### Against Fable

Fable is strong at clubs, community, feeds, and shared discussion.

Differentiation:

- Smaller and more private by default.
- Less feed-driven.
- More focused on weekly consistency and comeback behavior.
- No requirement that group identity centers on a shared book.

### Against Bookly And Basmo

Bookly and Basmo are strong personal reading trackers.

Differentiation:

- Social accountability is core, not an add-on.
- Group progress and encouragement matter as much as personal stats.
- AI should support reflection, not replace reading.
- The product should keep users returning to books, not to tracking for its own sake.

### Against Generic Habit Apps

Streaks, Loop Habit Tracker, Habitica, and Finch prove that habit loops can be engaging.

Differentiation:

- Reading-specific progress units.
- Book context.
- Optional reflection prompts.
- Social accountability around a shared identity as readers.
- Grace and comeback mechanics tuned for reading.

## Launch Channels

### Founder-Led Friend Groups

Best first channel.

Approach:

- Recruit small groups from existing personal networks.
- Ask each group to use the app for two to four weeks.
- Focus on friend groups that already talk about books or self-improvement.

Learning goal:

- Does the app help people keep reading when a normal group chat would fade?

### Campus And Young Professional Communities

Approach:

- Test with student clubs, honors groups, dorm friend groups, alumni circles, and young professional communities.
- Position as a low-pressure alternative to formal book clubs.

Learning goal:

- Which life stage and group type has the strongest activation and retention?

### BookTok, Bookstagram, And Reader Micro-Communities

Approach:

- Start with small creators and reader group admins, not broad influencer campaigns.
- Offer a private group challenge template.

Learning goal:

- Do social readers want private accountability, or are they mainly looking for discovery and recommendations?

### Existing Group Chats

Approach:

- Position Reading Momentum as a companion to a group chat rather than a replacement.
- Encourage one person to create a group and invite three to six friends.

Learning goal:

- Does a structured check-in layer create more reading follow-through than chat alone?

### Waitlist Or Early Access Page

Approach:

- Test message variants before building broad launch assets.
- Ask people whether they want solo tracking, private groups, challenges, or book clubs.

Learning goal:

- Which promise converts without overcommitting the product?

## Onboarding Messaging Hypotheses

### Hypothesis A: Friend Accountability

Message:

- Read more with friends, even when everyone picks a different book.

Why it may work:

- It directly explains the unusual group model.
- It separates Reading Momentum from traditional book clubs.

Risk:

- Users without an immediate group may not activate.

Metric:

- Invite sent rate during onboarding.

### Hypothesis B: Restart Reading

Message:

- Get back into reading with small weekly check-ins.

Why it may work:

- It speaks to dormant readers without shaming them.
- It sets a low-friction expectation.

Risk:

- It may sound too solo and underplay group value.

Metric:

- First check-in completion rate.

### Hypothesis C: Low-Pressure Book Club

Message:

- The book club for people who cannot commit to one shared book.

Why it may work:

- It is memorable and clearly differentiated.
- It targets users who like the idea of a book club but dislike rigid formats.

Risk:

- It may imply discussion meetings or club management features that are not in MVP.

Metric:

- Group creation rate and second-week group activity.

### Hypothesis D: Screen-Time Swap

Message:

- Spend more of your free time reading, with a little help from friends.

Why it may work:

- It connects reading to a common motivation without promising productivity transformation.

Risk:

- It may attract users looking for screen-time controls, which are not core MVP.

Metric:

- Reading session frequency after signup.

### Hypothesis E: Gentle Momentum

Message:

- Build reading momentum without harsh streak resets.

Why it may work:

- It reinforces the product name and product principle.
- It appeals to users who dislike shame-based habit apps.

Risk:

- It may be less concrete than friend-based messaging.

Metric:

- Week 2 retention after a missed day.

## First-Run Experience Implications

The first session should help a user do three things quickly:

1. Pick or enter a current book.
2. Set a small reading intention.
3. Join or create a private group.

Recommended onboarding sequence:

- Ask why the user wants to read more.
- Ask whether they are reading solo or with friends.
- Let them add one current book without requiring complete metadata.
- Let them choose a default progress unit: pages, minutes, chapters, percent, or sessions.
- Invite them to create or join a private group.
- Encourage one first check-in immediately.

Avoid onboarding friction:

- Do not require importing a full library.
- Do not require a review.
- Do not require a shared group book.
- Do not require notification permission before the user sees value.
- Do not explain every future feature.

## Launch Experiments

### Experiment 1: Private Group Challenge

Concept:

- Invite groups to try a two-week reading momentum challenge.

Test:

- Do users invite friends and check in repeatedly?

Primary metrics:

- Group creation rate
- Invite acceptance rate
- Week 2 group activity

### Experiment 2: Different Books, Same Group

Concept:

- Position the app around the ability for everyone to read their own book.

Test:

- Does this remove friction compared with shared-book clubs?

Primary metrics:

- Onboarding completion rate
- Group activation rate
- Qualitative feedback on flexibility

### Experiment 3: Comeback-Friendly Streaks

Concept:

- Emphasize grace days, weekly momentum, and comeback progress.

Test:

- Do users return after missing a day?

Primary metrics:

- Missed-day recovery rate
- Week 4 retention
- User sentiment about pressure

### Experiment 4: Reflection Prompt Hook

Concept:

- After a check-in, offer a short optional reflection prompt.

Test:

- Does reflection increase attachment without adding friction?

Primary metrics:

- Prompt response rate
- Check-in completion time
- Repeat check-in rate

## What Not To Promise

Do not promise:

- That Reading Momentum will replace Goodreads.
- That the app is a full book catalog or review network.
- That it includes ebook reading, audiobook playback, or library borrowing.
- That it will generate accurate book summaries.
- That AI will understand every book perfectly.
- That it will make users read every day.
- That it will guarantee habit formation.
- That groups must or will finish books together.
- That points prove someone is a better reader.
- That the app will reduce screen time automatically.
- That public communities, creator clubs, or institutional programs are available in MVP.
- That all competitor import tools are ready.
- That recommendations are a core first-version feature.

Safer promise:

- Reading Momentum helps small groups create a simple rhythm of reading check-ins, flexible goals, and supportive accountability.

## Launch Success Metrics

Primary MVP growth metrics:

- Signup to first check-in rate
- Signup to group creation or group join rate
- Invite sent rate
- Invite acceptance rate
- Week 2 active group retention
- Week 4 active reader retention
- Missed-day recovery rate

Quality metrics:

- Users report feeling encouraged rather than pressured.
- Users understand that they can read different books in the same group.
- Users can explain the momentum score in plain language.
- Users say the app helped them read when they otherwise might not have.

Avoid optimizing too early for:

- Total screen time
- Feed scroll depth
- Public follower count
- Number of reviews written
- Total pages only
- Total books only
